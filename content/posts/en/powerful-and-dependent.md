There are, roughly, two kinds of powerful people.

The first kind is powerful in themselves. They command money, or they can threaten you, or they can move others to act — economic weight, force, influence. This is the old power, the kind history books are written about. It is concentrated, personal, and scarce. Most people never have it.

The second kind is powerful because someone handed them a tool. This power is borrowed from technology, and it has quietly become almost universal. Nearly every human alive now has internet access. Give that person a free ChatGPT account and they can do, in an afternoon, work that in 2010 required a professional: write publishable copy, translate between languages they don't speak, draft contracts, debug code, produce a decent illustration. A teenager with a phone now wields capabilities that a fully staffed office did not have two decades ago.

That is a staggering democratization of power, and I don't want to be cynical about it. It is real, and it is mostly good. But borrowed power comes with a bill, and the bill is written in a word that English handles clumsily and French handles perfectly.

## Dépendance

In French, *dépendance* means two things at once, and the coincidence is the whole point.

The first meaning is **addiction**. A drug addict is *dépendant* — hooked, unable to stop, organized around the substance. The second meaning is **requirement**, a structural reliance: an outbuilding on an estate is a *dépendance*, and one system that cannot function without another *dépend de* it. Need and craving, wearing the same word.

Technology lives in exactly that overlap, and the double meaning is not a pun — it's a diagnosis.

Start with the personal register. The person empowered by free AI does not, as a rule, walk away from it. They can't, quite. Once you have written a month of emails with a model at your elbow, the blank page becomes harder, not easier. The skill you were augmenting quietly atrophies, and the augmentation becomes the thing you now require to perform at all. What arrived as a superpower settles in as a *dependence* in both senses simultaneously: you reach for it like a habit, and you genuinely need it to do what you can now no longer do without it. The power was real. So are the strings.

## The system can't quit either

Scale that up and it stops being about individuals.

Jean-Marc Jancovici, the French energy analyst, likes to point out that a single barrel of oil contains the equivalent of several years of human physical labor — that we are, all of us, surrounded by hundreds of invisible "energy slaves" doing our lifting, and that a civilization is essentially energy given shape. His harder point is the one that stings: we are not going to voluntarily give that up. An economy that has tasted that much leverage restructures itself around it completely, and the restructuring is not reversible by good intentions. The dependence becomes load-bearing.

Digital technology is following the same script, faster. Once a capability is out of Pandora's box, the economic system does not sample it politely. It structures itself around the maximum possible use, because the logic of the system is growth, and any tool that lets you produce more will be used to produce more, until it is used as much as it possibly can be. Nobody decides this. It's emergent, and it's nearly gravitational. Like the addict who cannot un-know the relief the drug provides, an economy cannot un-know a productivity multiplier. Having found it, it must use it, and use all of it.

The one thing that ever bends this curve is not virtue. It's *regulation*. We know how to clone mammals; we have known for decades. Human cloning is technically within reach and yet it is not a market, not an industry, not a line item — because we collectively drew a line and enforced it. The technology being possible did not make it ubiquitous. A deliberate constraint did that. It is one of the few proofs we have that "if it can be built, it will be deployed at maximum scale" is not actually a law of nature. It only feels like one when we decline to draw the line.

## Slower, more conscious, more ours

So where does that leave us, holding this borrowed power we increasingly can't put down?

I keep returning to Timothée Parrique, the degrowth economist, whose framing is the cleanest I've found: the choice is *ralentir ou périr* — slow down or perish — and degrowth is not about living with less but about living with *enough*. The current paradigm treats infinite growth on a finite planet as the goal and every new technology as fuel for it. That paradigm is the actual problem. AI didn't create it; AI is being poured into it, and the pouring is what makes AI dangerous, not the technology itself.

The way out is not to smash the tools. It's to change what they're in service of. A technology that is slow, conscious, and chosen — used deliberately, in service of taking care of one another rather than extracting maximum throughput from one another — is a completely different thing from the same technology metabolized by an economy that only knows how to accelerate. Same capability. Opposite meaning. The difference is entirely in whether we drew a line, and where.

Almost all of us are powerful now. The question the double meaning of *dépendance* forces on us is whether we are strong enough to stay powerful without becoming, in the other sense of the word, owned by the thing that made us so.
